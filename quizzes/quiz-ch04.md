## Question 2

The chapter defines LTV as the "gross margin version." Why does the chapter insist on using gross margin rather than revenue in the LTV calculation?

- A) Gross margin is easier to calculate than revenue
- B) Using revenue in LTV overstates the actual value of a customer because it ignores the cost of serving them; gross margin LTV reflects what actually flows toward covering fixed costs and profit
- C) Investors prefer gross margin LTV because it is always a larger number than revenue LTV
- D) Revenue figures are proprietary and cannot legally be disclosed in investor presentations
- E) Gross margin LTV is the standard defined by GAAP accounting rules for SaaS companies

<details>
<summary>Show Answer & Explanations</summary>

- A) ❌ Gross margin is actually more complex to calculate than revenue because it requires accurate COGS attribution — it is not chosen for simplicity.
- ⭐ B) ✅ The chapter insists on gross margin LTV because revenue LTV includes the cost of delivering the service, which means it overstates true economic value. If a customer pays $100/month but it costs $40/month to serve them, the $60 gross margin is what's actually available to contribute toward CAC recovery and fixed cost coverage. Using revenue inflates the LTV/CAC ratio artificially.
- C) ❌ Gross margin LTV is *lower* than revenue LTV (because gross margin < revenue). Investors who prefer gross margin LTV do so because it is more conservative and honest.
- D) ❌ Revenue disclosure rules vary, but there is no legal prohibition on using revenue in internal calculations. The chapter's argument is economic, not legal.
- E) ❌ GAAP does not specify LTV calculation methodology — this is a management accounting and investor reporting convention, not a GAAP requirement.

</details>

## Question 3

According to the chapter, what is the industry benchmark for a "healthy" LTV/CAC ratio in a SaaS or subscription business, and what does it indicate?

- A) LTV/CAC of 1:1, indicating the customer pays back their acquisition cost exactly once
- B) LTV/CAC of 3:1 or higher, indicating the customer generates at least three dollars of gross margin for every dollar spent to acquire them
- C) LTV/CAC of 10:1, indicating the business has zero customer acquisition costs due to viral growth
- D) LTV/CAC of 0.5:1, indicating a growth-at-all-costs phase where acquisition investment exceeds returns
- E) LTV/CAC has no standard benchmark because every industry has different economics

<details>
<summary>Show Answer & Explanations</summary>

- A) ❌ A 1:1 ratio means the business breaks even on customer acquisition with no return — not a healthy business; just recouping the cost of finding the customer.
- ⭐ B) ✅ The chapter identifies 3:1 as the standard benchmark: a customer should generate at least three times their acquisition cost in gross margin over their lifetime. Below 3:1 suggests CAC is too high or retention too short; significantly above 3:1 may suggest underinvestment in growth.
- C) ❌ A 10:1 ratio is exceptional (Figma's case study approaches this through PLG CAC compression) but is not the industry benchmark for a healthy business. It also does not mean zero CAC — it means CAC is very low relative to LTV.
- D) ❌ A 0.5:1 ratio means the business destroys value per customer acquired — this is not a "growth-at-all-costs" strategy, it is an unsustainable model without significant structural improvement.
- E) ❌ While ratios vary by industry, the chapter does present 3:1 as the widely accepted benchmark starting point for subscription businesses.

</details>

## Question 4

- A) AI companies have higher gross margins than traditional software because they require fewer employees
- B) As AI companies scale, their variable costs (API inference costs, GPU costs) grow with usage, compressing gross margins — unlike traditional SaaS where marginal cost of serving additional users approaches zero
- C) AI tools cause gross margin compression in their customer industries by automating previously expensive functions
- D) The gross margin squeeze refers to the declining price of AI APIs over time due to competition
- E) AI companies cannot report gross margin figures because their cost structures are classified as trade secrets

<details>
<summary>Show Answer & Explanations</summary>

- A) ❌ The chapter argues the opposite — AI companies face a structural gross margin challenge that traditional SaaS companies do not.
- ⭐ B) ✅ The chapter identifies this as a critical structural risk for AI-native businesses: unlike traditional SaaS (where the marginal cost of serving an additional user is nearly zero), AI inference costs (API calls, GPU compute) scale with usage. A company that is paying $0.01 per API call and growing rapidly faces a COGS line that grows proportionally with revenue — compressing gross margins below traditional SaaS levels.
- C) ❌ This describes AI's impact on *customer* industries, not the internal gross margin structure of AI companies themselves.
- D) ❌ API price compression is a real market trend but is distinct from the Gross Margin Squeeze Problem as defined in the chapter, which concerns the cost-volume relationship of AI inference.
- E) ❌ There is no legal constraint on AI companies reporting gross margins. This is fabricated.

## Question 7

The chapter warns that "monthly retention hides the truth." What does it recommend instead, and why?

- A) Weekly retention is more accurate than monthly and should replace it for all SaaS businesses
- B) Cohort analysis — tracking groups of customers who signed up in the same period to see how their retention evolves over time, revealing whether the business has a retention ceiling
- C) Annual renewal rates should replace monthly churn because they reflect true customer commitment
- D) Net Promoter Score (NPS) should replace retention as the primary health metric for subscription businesses
- E) Retention should be measured per product feature used, not per customer account

<details>
<summary>Show Answer & Explanations</summary>

- A) ❌ The chapter does not recommend weekly retention — the issue is not the frequency of measurement but the level of aggregation. Aggregate monthly rates mask cohort-level deterioration.
- ⭐ B) ✅ The chapter explicitly recommends cohort analysis as the solution to the aggregate monthly retention problem. When you look at a single average monthly churn rate, early cohorts with strong retention can mask deteriorating retention in newer cohorts. Cohort analysis reveals whether the business is improving or degrading at the customer level over time.
- C) ❌ Annual renewal rates have the same aggregation problem — they hide within-year behavior and don't reveal cohort-level trends.
- D) ❌ NPS is a customer satisfaction survey metric, not a retention measurement. The chapter discusses retention as the behavioral ground truth that NPS can only approximate.
- E) ❌ Feature-level retention is a product analytics metric, useful for product development, but not the replacement for account-level cohort retention analysis.

</details>

- A) Logo churn measures enterprise clients while revenue churn measures SMB clients
- B) Logo churn counts the number of customers who cancel; revenue churn measures the dollar value lost — a business can have high logo churn but low revenue churn if the churning customers are small accounts, while large accounts expand
- C) Revenue churn is always a larger number than logo churn, making it the more important metric
- D) Logo churn and revenue churn are interchangeable terms — they measure the same thing in different units
- E) Logo churn applies only to companies with brand sponsorship partnerships

<details>
<summary>Show Answer & Explanations</summary>

- A) ❌ Logo churn is not segmented by company size — it counts all account cancellations regardless of customer size.

A company losing many small customers (high logo churn) but retaining and expanding large accounts can show near-zero or even negative revenue churn — because expansion revenue from large accounts offsets logo losses. This is why both metrics are required for a complete churn diagnosis.
- C) ❌ Revenue churn is not always larger — in a PLG business where small logos churn but enterprise accounts expand, revenue churn can be negative (net revenue retention > 100%) even with positive logo churn.
- D) ❌ Logo churn and revenue churn are distinctly different metrics measuring different things; they are explicitly contrasted in the chapter as a diagnostic pair.
- E) ❌ "Logo" in this context means customer account/brand, not brand sponsorship. There is no connection to marketing sponsorship partnerships.
