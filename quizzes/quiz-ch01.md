## Question 1

According to Chapter 1, what is the key distinguishing characteristic of Large Language Models (LLMs) compared to prior AI systems such as spam filters and recommendation engines?

- A) LLMs are faster and cheaper to deploy than prior AI systems
- B) LLMs are trained on labeled datasets with clear right/wrong answers
- C) LLMs operate on unstructured human intent and produce novel outputs across open-ended domains
- D) LLMs are limited to a single domain and require retraining for each new task
- E) LLMs replaced all prior AI systems in enterprise use by 2020

<details>
<summary>Show Answer & Explanations</summary>

- A) ❌ Speed and cost are not the defining distinction — the chapter explicitly frames the shift as qualitative, not quantitative.
- B) ❌ This describes prior AI systems (spam filters, classifiers), not LLMs. LLMs do not rely on labeled right/wrong datasets for task execution.
- ⭐ C) ✅ The chapter explicitly states: "LLMs operate on *unstructured human intent* and produce *novel outputs* across domains." This is described as "a qualitative shift in the type of work that can be automated."
- D) ❌ This is the opposite of correct — prior AI systems required domain-specific retraining; LLMs transfer across domains with minimal prompting.
- E) ❌ The chapter dates the public emergence of this capability to November 2022 with ChatGPT's release, not 2020.

</details>

## Question 2

- A) Companies that move first into AI always gain a durable, unassailable competitive advantage
- B) Companies that move too early face high implementation costs and low model quality
- C) The optimal AI adoption strategy is to wait until 100% of competitors have adopted before acting
- D) Companies that move too late face structural competitive disadvantage
- E) The winner's curse applies only to companies with fewer than 500 employees

<details>
<summary>Show Answer & Explanations</summary>

- A) ❌ The chapter explicitly argues against this — first movers face the highest costs and the lowest model quality, not a guaranteed advantage.
- ⭐ B) ✅ The chapter states: "the companies that move first face the highest implementation costs and the lowest model quality." This is one side of the winner's curse paradox.
- C) ❌ The chapter recommends being "informed early majority" — not waiting until full adoption, which would leave a company at maximum competitive disadvantage.
- ⭐ D) ✅ The chapter states: "the companies that move last face the highest competitive disadvantage." This is the other side of the paradox.

## Question 6

- A) Being the absolute first company in any market to adopt AI tools
- B) Waiting until AI adoption is normalized before acting to minimize risk
- C) Moving after technology achieves sufficient reliability for production use, but before the late majority normalizes it
- D) Adopting only AI tools that have been approved by government regulators
- E) Letting competitors test AI first and copying only what they find successful

<details>
<summary>Show Answer & Explanations</summary>

- A) ❌ This describes the "Innovators" category in Rogers' diffusion framework — the chapter explicitly argues against this position as incurring highest costs and lowest model quality.
- B) ❌ This describes Late Majority adoption — the chapter warns this position faces maximum competitive disadvantage.
- ⭐ C) ✅ The chapter states: "The optimal adoption strategy is neither first-mover nor fast-follower in the classical sense. It is informed early majority: move after the technology has achieved sufficient reliability for production use, but before the late majority normalizes it."
- D) ❌ Regulatory approval is not mentioned as the threshold criterion for adoption timing in this framework.
- E) ❌ This is a passive fast-follower strategy. The chapter's "informed early majority" position requires active early engagement, not merely copying proven moves.

</details>
