## Question 3

When applying Filter 1 (Agreement) of the Three-Filter Test, the chapter warns about the "false positive trap." What does this mean?

- A) Founders mistake correlation data for causation in market research
- B) AI tools generate agreement signals that don't reflect real customer behavior
- C) If you describe the problem and ask "Is this a problem for you?", you get yes-answers that mean nothing — the filter requires unprompted naming of the problem
- D) Customers with false positives are more likely to churn after purchase
- E) Focus groups artificially inflate agreement rates because participants want to please moderators

<details>
<summary>Show Answer & Explanations</summary>

- A) ❌ The false positive trap is about interview methodology, not statistical research design or causation errors.
- B) ❌ AI tools are not the source of the false positive — the trap comes from how founders frame their interview questions.
- ⭐ C) ✅ The chapter states: "The agreement filter requires unprompted convergence. The customer must volunteer the problem before you name it." Describing the problem and asking for confirmation produces yes-answers contaminated by social desirability bias, as described in Fitzpatrick's *The Mom Test* framework cited in the chapter.
- D) ❌ Post-purchase churn is not what the "false positive trap" refers to in this context.
- E) ❌ While focus groups do have moderator effects, the chapter's false positive trap is specifically about one-on-one customer discovery where founders name the problem before the customer does.

</details>

## Question 5

The chapter analyzes the Cursor vs. GitHub Copilot case study. What does the chapter identify as the primary reason Cursor was able to gain significant market share despite GitHub Copilot's first-mover advantage?

- A) Cursor had a superior underlying AI model that GitHub Copilot could not access
- B) Cursor was significantly cheaper in monthly pricing than GitHub Copilot
- C) Cursor embedded AI at the IDE level — giving it full codebase context and multi-file awareness — creating deeper workflow integration and higher switching costs than a plugin overlay
- D) Cursor had better venture capital backing and brand marketing
- E) Cursor launched in a different geographic market and avoided direct competition initially

<details>
<summary>Show Answer & Explanations</summary>

- A) ❌ The chapter explicitly notes that "same models, different architectures" — both tools accessed equivalent frontier models. Model quality was not the differentiator.
- B) ❌ Pricing is not identified as the primary competitive factor in the chapter's moat analysis of this case study.
- ⭐ C) ✅ The chapter's moat analysis concludes that Cursor's advantage was architectural depth: "Cursor embedded AI at the IDE level, giving it access to the full codebase, multi-file awareness, and a conversational interface that could hold context across an entire project." This created deeper workflow integration and higher switching costs than Copilot's plugin approach.
- D) ❌ The chapter focuses the moat analysis on product architecture and workflow depth, not marketing or funding.
- E) ❌ Both products launched in the same English-language developer market — geographic segmentation was not the strategy.

</details>

- C) Filter 2 (Scale) — because the initial market calculation only yielded ~$2.8M ARR at 5% penetration
- D) Filter 1 (Agreement) — because designers disagreed about what the core problem was
- E) Filter 3 (Competence) — because no proprietary data was available

<details>
<summary>Show Answer & Explanations</summary>

## Question 8

- A) First-mover timing advantage in an emerging market
- B) Superior venture capital relationships and funding access
- C) Distribution — direct access to the customer segment through prior employer networks, credentials, or relationships
- D) Lower pricing than all existing competitors
- E) A larger engineering team than the competition

<details>
<summary>Show Answer & Explanations</summary>

- A) ❌ Timing advantage (first-mover) is a separate strategic concept; the chapter's Filter 3 forms focus on structural, durable advantages, not timing.
- B) ❌ VC relationships are not listed among the four competence forms in the chapter — they may help, but they are not an asymmetric advantage from the framework's perspective.
- ⭐ C) ✅ Distribution is explicitly listed as one of four competence forms: "You have direct access to the customer segment in question — through prior employer networks, industry associations, professional credentials, or existing relationships. Distribution is the single most undervalued advantage in early-stage startups."
- D) ❌ Lower pricing is a competitive tactic, not an "unfair advantage" in the competence sense — any well-funded competitor can undercut on price.

</details>

## Question 10

The chapter's "hired and fired" interview technique is derived from which framework, and what is the core insight it operationalizes?

- A) Porter's Five Forces; customers hire products to reduce competitive threats
- B) Jobs to Be Done (Christensen); customers hire products to make progress on a specific job — and fire them when a better-suited solution becomes available
- C) The Business Model Canvas; customers hire products that address all nine BMC blocks simultaneously
- D) Diffusion of Innovations (Rogers); customers hire products at different stages of the adoption curve
- E) The Lean Startup (Ries); customers hire MVPs to provide validated learning feedback

<details>
<summary>Show Answer & Explanations</summary>

- A) ❌ Porter's Five Forces is a competitive industry analysis framework, not a customer motivation framework. The chapter does not use it here.
- ⭐ B) ✅ The chapter explicitly attributes the "hired and fired" technique to Clayton Christensen's Jobs to Be Done framework, introduced in *The Innovator's Solution* (2003) and expanded in *Competing Against Luck* (2016). The core insight: "customers don't buy products — they hire outcomes," and fire products when a better-suited solution for the same job emerges.
- C) ❌ The BMC is used in Chapter 1 as a disruption map; it is not the basis of the hired-and-fired interview technique.
- D) ❌ Rogers' Diffusion of Innovations describes adoption timing, not why individual customers choose and abandon specific products.
- E) ❌ The Lean Startup is a product development methodology; the hired-and-fired technique is a customer research and motivation-mapping tool, not a validation framework.

</details>
